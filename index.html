Introduction to Modeling Theory

Understanding Models and Their Applications

Definition of Modeling

Modeling is the process of building, studying, and applying models to understand systems better.

Key Concept: A model substitutes the original object for study under certain assumptions and hypotheses.

Example: Using a scale model of a building to study architectural designs.

Types of Models

  • Cognitive Models: Reflect existing relations and connections (e.g., Newton's laws).
  • Pragmatic Models: Used for organizing practical actions (e.g., traffic light systems).
  • Instrumental Models: Assist in building and studying other models (e.g., simulation software).

Classification by Degree of Abstraction

  • Material Models: Physical replicas (e.g., full-scale, quasi full-scale, scale, analog models).
  • Ideal Models: Theoretical representations (e.g., intuitive and symbolic models).
  • Symbolic Models: Linguistic, visual, graphical, and mathematical models (e.g., flowcharts).

Classification by Stability

  • Stable Models: Return to original state after disturbance (e.g., pendulum returning to rest).
  • Unstable Models: Disturbances intensify over time (e.g., economic systems with growing inflation).

Relation to External Factors

  • Open Models: Affected by external variables (e.g., weather forecasting models).
  • Closed Models: Function independently of external influences (e.g., internal feedback systems).

Relation to Time

  • Static Models: Do not involve time parameters (e.g., structural models of buildings).
  • Dynamic Models: Include time as a parameter (e.g., population growth models).

Mathematical Model Classification

  • Hierarchy Level: Microlevel, macrolevel, metalevel (e.g., macrolevel models describe technological processes).
  • Nature of Properties: Structural and functional (e.g., structural models describe trophic structures).
  • Representation Method: Analytical, numerical, algorithmic, simulation (e.g., algorithmic models use algorithms).
  • Acquisition Method: Theoretical and empirical (e.g., empirical models derived from experiments).
  • Form of Representation: Logical, set-theoretical, graph (e.g., graph models represent relationships between elements).
  • Content of Irregular Components: Deterministic and stochastic (e.g., stochastic models like queueing systems).

Stages of Model Development

  • Requirement Analysis and Design: Statement and analysis of goals, collection of initial data, building and checking a conceptual model.
  • Model Development: Selection of modeling environment, development of logical model, setting simulation time, model verification.
  • Experimentation: Simulation run, parameter variation, statistics collection, result analysis.
  • Finalizing Results: Assessment and comparison of goals vs. results, final report preparation.

Simulation Time Advancement

  • Event-Driven: Advances time based on event occurrences (e.g., discrete-event simulations).
  • Time-Stepped: Advances time in fixed intervals (e.g., annual economic forecasts).

Conclusion

- Models simplify complex systems for study.

- Various classifications help choose appropriate models.

- Stages of model development ensure thorough analysis.

- Importance of modeling in understanding and predicting real-world phenomena.
